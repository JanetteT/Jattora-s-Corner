Chicxulub Crater

What happened?

    The Chicxulub Crater is an impact structure formed by the collision of a meteorite on the Yucatán Peninsula in Mexico, around 66 million years ago. It was 10 km in size and had a speed of 20 km/s.

    The impact vaporized thousands of rocks. It struck the Yucatán Peninsula, Mexico, near Chicxulub Pueblo.

    It left an enormous crater nearly 200 km wide and generated an initial tsunami with waves 1.5 km high. And subsequent tsunamis with waves over 100 m tall.

    The ejected material was sulfur-rich gypsum and water which caused a nuclear winter Nearly 70% of all species died out, including the majority of dinosaurs.

    Climate change following the impact was the cause of the mass extinction.

    The Chicxulub crater is the remnant of that impact.

How was it Discovered?

    The Chicxulub crater was covered by younger deposits of limestones due to erosion and geological activity. Because it was partially offshore, nobody could actually see the structure.

    In 1980, scientists Luis and Walter Alvarez discovered a layer rich in Iridium. This element is incredibly rare on Earth, but common in asteroids. Their research led to a stunning conclusion: this iridium layer was the geological signature of the Cretaceous-Paleogene mass extinction.

    Separately, other geophysicists noticed a distinct symmetrical magnetic anomaly on survey maps of the Yucatán Peninsula. In the center of this anomaly, they found minerals that had been instantly modified by extreme heat and pressure.

    The initial surveys that hinted at the crater's existence weren't looking for an impact site at all—they were just searching for oil.
